Act 2 — How Atoms Work

A Tiny Solar System

⬡

Nucleus: protons + neutrons
Cloud: electrons in orbit

Every atom is a tiny solar system. At the center: a nucleus packed with protons and neutrons. Orbiting around it: a cloud of electrons.

The number of protons—that's the atomic number, Z—defines what element you have. Hydrogen has 1. Carbon has 6. Gold has 79.

Incredible Density

As Z increases, the nucleus gets denser.

More protons. More neutrons. Same tiny space.

Element 118: 230 billion kg/cm³

The density of a neutron star—inside an atom.

As Z increases, something remarkable happens. The nucleus gets denser. By element 118, it's about 230 billion kilograms per cubic centimeter. That's neutron star density.

Act 3 — The War Inside

The Problem: Coulomb Repulsion

Protons are all positively charged.

Positive charges repel each other.

Repulsion ∝ Z²

Double the protons → quadruple the repulsion

But here's the problem. Protons are all positively charged. And positive charges repel each other. This is Coulomb repulsion—and it scales as Z squared.

The Hero: Strong Nuclear Force

←
⬡⬡
→

Most powerful force in nature
But only works at ~1 femtometer

What holds it together? The strong nuclear force. It's the most powerful force in nature—but it only works at extremely short range. About one femtometer.

The Breaking Point

In large nuclei:

  • Distant protons barely feel the strong force
  • But they definitely feel Coulomb repulsion
💥

Eventually, the nucleus tears itself apart.

As nuclei get larger, protons on opposite sides can barely feel each other's strong force—but they definitely feel the Coulomb repulsion. Eventually, the nucleus tears itself apart.

But here's what most people miss: this only works because deuterium and tritium are simple. Two or three nucleons each. Try to fuse heavy elements and Coulomb repulsion makes it nearly impossible.

Act 5 — The Oscillation Key

Smashing → Resonance

Nuclei can vibrate—like a drop of water oscillating.

So if brute force can't work, what about resonance? This is where things get fascinating. Nuclei can vibrate—like a drop of water oscillating in slow motion.

Frequency: 10-20 MHz (heavy nuclei)

This is called the Giant Dipole Resonance. The protons slosh one way, the neutrons slosh the other. It happens at specific frequencies—around 10 to 20 megahertz for heavy nuclei.
